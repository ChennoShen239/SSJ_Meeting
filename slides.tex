\section{Sequence Space v.s State Space}

\begin{frame}{Sequence Space v.s State Space}{Motivation}

    In heterogeneous-agent models, the main difficulty is that \textbf{the economy’s state includes the whole cross-sectional distribution}.


    Sequence-space methods \emph{avoid} carrying this object around explicitly when studying \textbf{local dynamics around the steady state}.




\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Sequence Space v.s State Space}{State Space}

    \begin{definition}
        A \alert{state-space representation} describes the economy recursively.
        At date $t$, a state variable $x_t$ summarizes all payoff-relevant information from the past, and the model evolves according to
        \[
            x_{t+1}=f(x_t,\varepsilon_{t+1}),
            \qquad
            y_t=g(x_t).
        \]
        The key idea is: \emph{once $x_t$ is known, the future no longer depends on the past separately.}
    \end{definition}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Sequence Space v.s State Space}{Sequence Space}

    \begin{definition}
        A \alert{sequence-space representation} treats equilibrium as a mapping between entire time paths of endogenous and exogenous variables. We solve for sequences
        \(
        \{X_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty}
        \)
        such that the equilibrium conditions hold at all dates:
        \[
            F\left(\{X_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty},\{Z_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty}\right)=0,
        \]
        where $X$ is the sequence of endogenous variables and $Z$ is the sequence of shocks or policy paths.
    \end{definition}


    \textbf{Intuition:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item State space asks: \emph{what is the economy's current state, and how does it transition?}
        \item Sequence space asks: \emph{given a path of shocks, what equilibrium path of outcomes clears the economy?}
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Sequence Space vs.\ State Space}{Comparison}

    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
    \begin{tabular}{p{0.20\textwidth} p{0.36\textwidth} p{0.36\textwidth}}
        \hline
         & \textbf{State space}                                            & \textbf{Sequence space} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Unknowns}
         & current state $x_t$
         & entire paths $\{X_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty}$                                                     \\

        \textbf{Core object}
         & $x_{t+1}=f(x_t,\varepsilon_{t+1})$
         & $F\left(\{X_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty},\{Z_t\}_{t=0}^{\infty}\right)=0$                           \\

        \textbf{Linearization}
         & around current state / transition law
         & around steady-state perfect-foresight paths                                               \\

        \textbf{Main bottleneck}
         & high-dimensional state, esp.\ the distribution
         & large Jacobians, but easy composition/inversion                                           \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}

\end{frame}



\begin{frame}{Sequence Space v.s State Space}{Takeaways}

    When the distribution is the hard state variable, sequence space often turns a “huge state” problem into a “structured Jacobian” problem.


\end{frame}
